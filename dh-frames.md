<details markdown="1">
  <summary>Why is 'minimal computing' here too?</summary>

- Minimal computing isn't just about being thrifty with computational resources. It's also about thinking holistically about labour, infrastructure, environmental impact, power, diversity, equity, and inclusion.
- It asks what is necessary, for whom, at what cost, and what are the wider implications (rather than just assuming that more data, more speed, and more scale are better ... or that less is always better!)
- There can also sometimes be trade-offs between accessibility and certain kinds of minimalism: highly optimised, lightweight systems may exclude users who rely on assistive technologies, while feature-rich platforms can increase energy use and technical complexity.
</details>

## Interoperability {#formats-standards}

What systems can talk to what systems, and how easily? Sometimes this can be a very literal, technical question. Has this data been prepared according to a particular standard? It also opens up ethical and political questions. How do human systems interact with data generated by the more-than-human world? How do different types of human knowledge (including Indigenous knowledges) interact? 

<details markdown="1"><summary>What is mark-up?</summary>
"Mark-up" often means adding information to a text that explains what parts of it are, in a way a computer can understand. For example, HTML marks up how content is displayed on the web (headings, paragraphs, links); XML marks up the underlying structure and meaning of data in a flexible, machine-readable way; and TEI is a specific scholarly XML standard for marking up literary and historical texts in rich, interpretive detail.

- [XML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XML) – a structured way of marking up text so computers can tell what different parts *are* (e.g. titles, dates, names)
- [XSLT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XSLT) / [SQL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SQL) – XSLT reshapes XML documents into new forms; SQL asks questions of databases (e.g. “show me all items from 1850”)
- [JSON](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JSON) – JSON is a common format for storing and transmitting structured data
- [SPARQL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SPARQL) – a query language for linked data across the web
- [TEI](https://tei-c.org/) – a scholarly XML standard for encoding literary and historical texts in detail
- [IIIF](https://iiif.io/) – a shared framework for libraries and museums to deliver high-resolution images online so they can be viewed, compared, and reused across sites
</details>

<details markdown="1"><summary>What is sustainability?</summary>

- For some time, 'sustainability' in a Digital Humanities context tended to be about long-term preservation - how do you ensure data remains accessible as technology and institutions change, etc. Nowadays, sustainability tends to mean environmental sustainability (the two things are connected).
- In a broader sense, sustainability is sometimes divided into environmental, social, and economic sustainability, and linked with sustainable development, corporate sustainability (CSR, ESG etc.), regulation and compliance, and the UN Sustainable Development Goals.
- Sustainability can be a broad umbrella term for many ways of thinking about climate, nature, the environment, the more-than-human world, etc. At the same time, sustainability as a concept is sometimes critiqued. For example, post-development and pluriversal perspectives offer critiques of sustainable development, as do some degrowth / postgrowth perspectives. Some design perspectives see sustainability as a stepping stone to something better, e.g. regenerative design.

## What is data science? {#intro-to-stats}

DH+ exists at the crossroads of media, arts, and humanities, and data science / computer science.

<details markdown="1">
<summary>What are all the terms I need to know to understand basic statistics?</summary>

- Variable: In this context, it means some measurable characteristic that can vary. For example, in a student survey, 'age,' 'favourite subject,' and 'hours spent studying per week' could all be variables. Statistics studies variation. A variable is anything that varies.
- Distribution: The pattern of values a variable takes across a dataset. This shows how common or rare different values are. For example, if most people earn between £20,000 and £30,000, but a few earn over £200,000, then the income distribution will be very skewed. Understanding the distribution matters because relying only on averages can hide inequality, clustering, outlier values, or other interesting things. Some other examples:
  - You might have heard of a 'normal' distribution. This means the values cluster around a central average in a bell-shaped curve: most cases are near the middle, and very high and very low values become steadily rarer. The heights of a group of adults will probably make a kind of bell curve.
 - Uniform distribution: all outcomes in a range are about equally common, so the distribution is relatively flat (e.g. rolling a fair die: 1–6 are equally likely).
 - Clustered: the values bunch into one or more dense clumps rather than spreading smoothly across a range (e.g. survey responses on political ideology might cluster around “centre-left” and “centre-right” positions rather than filling every point evenly).
 - Bimodal: there are two clear peaks, often because you are mixing two groups or two seasons/processes together (e.g. daily temperatures across a whole year in a place with cold winters and warm summers can form two peaks, one winter peak and one summer peak).
- Mean (Average): The sum of all values divided by the number of things / observations / whatever it may be. For example, if five ninjas score 60, 65, 70, 75, and 90 on a test, the mean gives a single number that represents their overall performance. It is useful but can be misleading when extreme values pull it upward or downward. By contrast, the 'median' is the middle value when you sort the data from lowest to highest (or if there are an even number of values, then it is the average of the two middle values). The 'mode' is the most common value (or values) in the dataset.
- Standard Deviation: When you have a set of values, you can calculate the standard deviation. This is a measure of how spread out values are around the mean. Larger values indicate more variability. For example, if everyone in an extreme saxophone competition scores between 68 and 72, the standard deviation is small. But if scores range from 30 to 95, it is large. This matters because two groups might have the same average, but still have very different levels of consistency or inequality. The way to calculate the standard deviation is first to find the variance, then take the square root.
- Variance: A measure of how spread out values are around the mean, calculated as the average of the squared deviations from the mean. For example, if everyone in a class scores almost the same mark, the variance is small; if scores range widely, the variance is large. Variance matters because it quantifies variability, but since it is measured in squared units, it is mainly useful as a stepping stone to standard deviation. Te calculate variance, first find the mean. Then subtract the mean from each number (these are the “differences”). Then square each difference (so negatives don’t cancel positives). Then find the average of those squared differences. Obviously there are tools that help to automate this!
- Sample: The subset of individuals or cases actually observed in a study. For example, if you survey 500 voters to understand national opinion, those 500 people are your sample. 
- Population: The entire group you want to draw conclusions about. In the same example, all eligible voters in the country form the population. Keeping the population in mind clarifies the scope of your claims and prevents inflated conclusions.
- Sampling Bias: A systematic distortion that occurs when some members of the population are more likely to be included than others. For example, an online survey shared only on a mailing list for fans of the cartoon The Racoons may overrepresent Gen X and older Millenials.
- Confidence Interval: A confidence interval is a range that shows how much your estimate might reasonably vary, because you only looked at a sample, not the whole population. For example, suppose you survey 1,000 people and find that 52% support a new law about sourdough starters. Because you did not ask everyone in the country, your result will not be perfectly exact. If you took a different random sample of 1,000 people, you might get 49%, or 51%, or 53%. Different samples give slightly different answers just by chance. So what does a 95% confidence interval of 48%–56% mean? It basically means this: using this standard method, if you repeatedly took new random samples of 1,000 people and calculated an interval each time in the same way, about 95 out of every 100 of those intervals would contain the true level of support in the whole population. About 5 out of 100 would miss it. In other words, 48%–56% is the range of values that are reasonably consistent with the data you observed, given normal sampling variation. (It doesn't mean there is a 95% chance the true value lies in this specific interval. It means the method used to construct the interval captures the true value about 95% of the time in the long run).
- Correlation: A measure of the strength and direction of association between two variables, without implying that one causes the other. For example, eating delicious ice cream and getting sunburnt may be correlated because both increase in hot weather, but one does not cause the other. 
- Causal Inference: The process of determining whether and how one variable produces changes in another rather than merely being associated with it.
- Dataset: A structured collection of data. It's often organised in rows (cases) and columns (variables). For example, a spreadsheet where each row represents one survey respondent and each column records their age, income, and political preference is a dataset. 
- Model: A simplified mathematical or computational representation of relationships in data, used to explain patterns or make predictions. Often in data science it might mean a model trained via Machine Learning methods (but it doesn't always mean that).
- Square: A number multiplied by itself. Squaring can be important in statistics because it removes negative signs and gives extra weight to larger differences.
- Square root: The inverse of squaring. The square root of x is a number that, when multiplied by itself, produces x as the result.
- Statistical Power: Power is the estimated probability that a study will detect a real effect, if that effect truly exists. A study with low power might fail to find an effect even when there is one (a false negative). Power increases with larger sample sizes, larger true effects, and lower noise. Underpowered studies often produce unreliable or misleading conclusions.
- Randomisation: Randomisation means assigning participants to groups by chance (for example, flipping a coin to decide who receives a new treatment). This helps ensure that differences between groups are not due to pre-existing characteristics. 
- Control Group: A control group is a group that does not receive the treatment or intervention being tested. For example, if one group receives a new teaching method, the control group continues with the usual method. The control group provides a baseline for comparison, helping isolate the effect of the treatment.
- Confounding Variable: A confounder is a third variable that influences both the supposed cause and the outcome, creating a misleading association. For example, ice cream sales and sunburn are correlated, but hot weather is the confounder causing both.
- Regression: A statistical method used to estimate relationships between variables. When you run a regression analysis, you are trying to find out how one thing is related to another, especially when you want to predict a number. In regression, you usually have an outcome (the thing you want to explain) and one or more predictors (the things you think might influence it). For example, you might use regression to estimate how a person’s housing costs (the outcome) are related to their income, region, and whether they rent or own (the predictors). The method draws a “best-fitting” line (or curve) through the data to summarise the overall pattern. In simple linear regression, this is literally a straight line showing how the outcome tends to change as one predictor increases. More complex forms allow several predictors at once, or curved relationships rather than straight lines. Importantly, regression shows patterns of association in the data — it does not by itself prove that one thing causes another.
- Feature: In data science, a feature is a measurable input used by a model to make predictions. Features are often variables derived from raw data. For example, in predicting house prices, features might include number of bedrooms, floor area, neighbourhood, or distance to a train station. Choosing, transforming, or engineering features is an important part of modelling, because what the model can learn depends on what information it is given.
- Classification: A type of modelling task where the goal is to assign cases to categories. For example, a model might classify emails as “spam” or “not spam,” or predict whether an application to become a goblin is “approved” or “denied.” Classification models estimate the probability that a case belongs to each category, then assign it to the most likely one.
- Overfitting: When a model learns the training data too closely, including random noise or accidental patterns, rather than capturing general relationships. An overfitted model may perform extremely well on the data it was trained on but poorly on new, unseen data. Overfitting is a risk especially with complex models and small datasets. Techniques such as cross-validation, regularisation, or using separate training and test sets help detect and reduce overfitting.

</details>
